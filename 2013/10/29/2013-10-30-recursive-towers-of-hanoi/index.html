Recursive Towers of Hanoi

2013-10-29
College Relics

It appears to me that the purpose of this exercise is to make us think of the link between induction and recursion. While induction attempts to prove that the next step P(n + 1) of some representative number P(n) is true, recursion defines that next step (n + 1) in terms of the previous step. It seems to me that induction authenticates the formula which recursion uses to define the progression or sequence.

To determine the number of moves required to complete the puzzle, we begin with the most fundamental base case, that of one disc. In this first case the number of moves F(n) equals the number of discs (n) = 1.

Next we move up to two discs (1 + 1). Our first two moves land each of the two discs on separate pegs. A third move will be required so the number of moves F(n) can no longer equal the number of discs. This obviously completes in three moves so perhaps the recursive relation for the number of moves, F(n) = the numnber of discs + 1.

We proceed to three discs to test out hypothesis, F(n) = n + 1. Go: 1) Small disc to the right 2) medium disc far right. 3) small disc on medium disc 4) large disc to the right 5) Alarm! Buzzer sounds. Five moves is too many to fit our hypothesis. F(n) = n + 1 clearly is not the solution. But we do complete the puzzle in 8 moves this time.

As others in the forum have already evidenced, 15 moves are required for four discs.

So we build a table and begin to see a pattern. 1) 1 disc; 1 move 2) 2 discs; 3 moves 3) 3 discs; 8 moves 4) 4 discs; 15 moves. We derive a formula that solves all three of these equations: F(n) =(2^n) - 1. Will this work for 5,6,7 or n+1 discs? We need to test with induction.

Testing by induction: Let’s say we have a lot of discs on the first peg, > 50. Let’s say we have relocated ALL OF THEM EXCEPT FOR ONE to peg 2. By assumption, the number of moves required to move ALL BUT ONE DISC = (2^n) -1. Then we move that big last disc to the empty peg. This requires ONE MOVE. Then we proceed to relocate all the remaining neatly stacked discs and by assumption this relocation upon that one large disc will require (2^n) - 1 moves. So, the number of steps required to move (n + 1) discs = (2^n) + 1 + (2^n). The one you see in the middle correlates to the one big disc we moved in the process above. So, (2^n) + 1 + (2^n) = 2 * (2^n) - 1 = 2^n+1 - 1. Therefore, we have proven F(n+1) is true. The recursive relation is 2^n - 1.

Lastly, why is this a recursive relation? Everytime you remove a disc from the original stack, you create, in essence, a new puzzle with the original number of discs minus 1: (n - 1). In fact, it doesn’t matter how many you remove, the formula (2^n) - 1 will still apply for the remaining (n) number of discs on the peg. It is almost as though induction proves all the possibilites going forward while recursion works on all the possibilities going backwards.
