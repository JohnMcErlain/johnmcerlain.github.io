UPC Development

2013-10-23
College Relics

In 1970, George Laurer, the developer of the Universal Product Code (UPC), was given an assignment by his boss at IBM to authenticate a bar code design by RCA. It was to be presented to the UGPCC (Uniform Grocery Product Code Council). There were other large companies competing for this contract including Singer, National Cash Register and Pitney Bowes. This assignment was due in just two weeks but the boss immediately went on vacation. When he returned he was not happy to find that George had created a completely new design. The RCA version was a round design with concentric circles. George reasoned that the RCA version was too large for one of the specs: the label had to fit in a 1.5 inch square area. Moreover, RCA’s version had only ten digits and George felt as though a minimum twelve digits were necessary to cover all the necessary information plus include a “check digit”. There is a relatively simple formula for the check digit which alerts the operator if the code has been misread.

Though I do not have the UGPCC specs, the basics are that Bar Codes contain information for the manufacturer’s ID, item price, item inventory, coupons, random weight items, pharaceutical items,and programmable reserved information digits. A device known as a scanner decodes the bars and sends the information to a central computer which sends back to the POS (point of sale) the correct price. This allows fraudulent store operations to jack the price while the shopper is still in the store prior to check-out. These operations have been dubbed as ‘Scanner Scammers’.
UPC technology rapidly spread from grocery stores to other retail stores and the shipping industry. Users must apply to the Uniform Code Council (UCC) which is the successor to the UGPCC. They pay an annual fee for the service.

The basics of the Laurer design went like this:

0 = 3-2-1-1;
1 = 2-2-2-1;
2 = 2-1-2-2;
3 = 1-4-1-1;
4 = 1-1-3-2;
5 = 1-2-3-1;
6 = 1-1-1-4;
7 = 1-3-1-2;
8 = 1-2-1-3;
9 = 3-1-1-2;

Each of the ten numbers to the left of the equal sign (above) are represented by four visible bars on the label. The numbers to the right of the equal sign represent relative thickness of the black or white bars of the bar code. For example, number “4” represents bars of width 1 (the thinnest of the bar widths) followed by another ‘width 1’, followed by a ‘width 3’ and last, by a ‘width 2’.

On a typical bar code label, the actual digits are printed (for we humans) below the bars they depict. All bar codes begin with a black-white-black combination after which the individual code begins. Each of the left most six digits have a white-black-white-black pattren. In the middle are three black and three white spacer bars. Each of the right most six digits have a black-white-black-white pattern. All added up, there are sixty total black and white bar on a typical bar code. There are also shorter bar code versions called zero suppressed numbers.

The scanned bars are encoded into digit streams of “1’s” and “0’s” but these are not binary numbers. They are bit representations of the above bar code representations which of course can be processed by a binary machine aka a computer.

Since not a single number in the system, when scanned from left to right,is the same as any number other number in the system when scanned from right to left (and the converse therefore applies as well) the system allows scanning from both directions with directional information. The bit stream of the first six numbers is the inverse of the bit stream of the second six digits (left to right).

Getting back to the George Laurer story, before his presentation, his boss further reprimanded him because the UGPCC was adamant about their desire to have a ten digit code. That was an easy fix as George figured they could “hide” two digits, one at each end of the bar code. To this day, that is why you see a small digit at the beginning and one at the end of a bar code, with ten larger digits in between. Ultimately, it worked well for George as his design was accepted; rumor has it he got a pat on the back and his boss got a promotion!
