Mobile Operating Systems Compared (Project)

2014-08-29
College Relics

Once upon a time, not so long ago, application users would be tethered to a desktop program, and perform ‘work’ such as creating a document or spreadsheet. We are now in a world in which we simply select the right “app” to get the job done for us. Moreover, there is an increasing usage of web services, offering data of all types to any developer with an imagination. Mobile operating system vendors have leveraged the innovation of a legion of independent developers who write apps to run atop vendor platforms.

Until Google came along with the Android platform in 2007, the outright leader in mobile operating system development had been Apple Inc. with their iOS (I operating system). Google threw gasoline on the fire of open source development when they unleashed a revamped Android 6 years ago and by 2013 possessed 81% market share of the mobile phone market. This was up 6% from a year earlier. IOS had basically taken a nose dive (since 2008) to 13.4% and Microsoft, reinventing their mobile platform with Windows 8.1, clawed its way into a distant third place with 4.1% market share (Android tops 81 percent of smartphone market share in Q3, 2013).

Whereas Windows and Apple maintained proprietary systems, Android was based on the open source Linux system. This enabled Google to leverage a legion of software developers. Currently on Github, Android app repositories number 133,052 followed by Windows Phone apps with 3190 and IOS with a paltry 436. Worldwide there are over 4 billion smart phones in operation. That number is expected to more than double in less than four years. The trends are salient as an Internet of phones connected to an Internet of things looms at our doorstep.
