OS Performance Issues

2014-08-10
College Relics

Increasing job throughput using virtual memory is possible when adequate RAM is available for the job or jobs at hand and minimal CPU resources are used for page swapping. However, when CPU processing cycles exceed a certain threshold, as they might with a large CPU intensive application, page swapping becomes more pronounced, then the CPU may become CPU bound, and exceeding CPU cycles are used to monitor page frames, and performance will suffer. Other factors that may affect the critical threshold of CPU usage would be the extent of fragmentation of the disk, saturation of the disk (resulting in thrashing) and the size of the swap file.

Availability and Reliability are statistical measures based on a percentage of time. ‘Availability’ measures the percentage of time a system, or component of a system will be ready for use. ‘Reliability’ measures the probability a system or component of a system will “NOT” fail in given time period. Both measures can be related using the common variable “MTBF” (mean time before failure). For instance, if disk drive had a MTBF of 1 hour and it took 1 hour to repair the drive, its availability would only be 50%. Therefore, in any given one hour period, the drive would have reliability of .135 or about 14 percent. This is quite low and would be quite unacceptable to any systems admin. Furthermore, two such drives used together would have a reduced reliability of less than 2 % (.14*.14).

Generally, devices are made to last longer than 1 hour and more likely to have MTBF on the order of thousands of hours. For example, a disk drive having an MTBF of 2000 hours with repair time of 4 hours would be available 99.8 of the time when new. However, after the drive was in service for a period of time, that number would decrease, at first very slightly for most its life; in the last 100 hours or so its availability would decrease dramatically. Correspondingly, its reliability would also diminish dramatically, going from over 99% most of its life to less than 50% in the last 100 hours of its life.

Because systems are comprised of many components for which manufacturers publish the respective MTBF stats, these numbers become valuable to systems administrators who can use them to track component life expectancies to assure maximum system up-time. Therefore tracking the ‘time’ a component has already been available can be used to update its MTBF to compute a current reliability figure.

Prior to launching a browser, the following usage numbers were observed and remained fairly consistent over the first few minutes of a cold start-up of my Windows 8.1 laptop:

The Task Manager was the only program I had selected, and 46 background processes and 27 Windows processes were running. Upon starting Windows Word (to write this assignment), only 1 background process added to the list. After several minutes, the disk came down from 99% accessing time to an average of under 10%. CPU usage also subsided to hover around 1-3% with spikes when I would command a file. RAM remained constant at about 40% usage (1.6/3.9 GB). Apparently, the operating system maintains a threshold around 40% after which paging, or demand paging occurs with the disk.

Now, the numbers are replayed after launching Chrome browser: now 53 background processes and 27 Windows processes are running. I can identify 6 Chrome processes but do not know what the extra process is. While some of the processes have familiar names such as Adobe, Microsoft and Chrome, many do not. Runtime Broker or Igfx Tray Module are two of them I have no idea what process or function they perform. Since most were only present at start-up with Task Manager running, they must be affiliated with the kernal or Task manager.

By not knowing the processes and program affiliations, a systems manager is not in complete control of knowing what is running in the system. This may lead to security vulnerability to hackers. One potential solution would be to develop a program that would screen processes before queuing them. This might further be refined by providing screening for only processes coming through the firewall ports.
