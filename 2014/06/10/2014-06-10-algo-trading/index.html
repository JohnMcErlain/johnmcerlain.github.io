Algo Trading

2014-06-10
College Relics

Stock market and financial futures exchanges, as well as a myriad of brokerage houses offer web services to disseminate price quotes to buying and selling participants world wide. These networks handle trillions of dollars of transactions yearly. Due to the money involved, the networks designed to support the financial industry are spared no expense.

Not too long ago, most stock market transactions were conducted on the floor of an exchange such as The New York Stock exchange (NYSE). Financial futures contracts were conducted in a “pit” using an open outcry system where traders would litterally be screaming (and spitting) at each other to fill customer orders. Sometimes, fist fights would break out. Nonetheless, it was considered a very efficient system, one which lasted almost two centuries; yet in a period of one decade was completely abolished and relaced by high speed networks. Today, when you see politicians grouped with NYSE personal ringing the open or closing bell, you are viewing “window candy”…that view of the stock exchange is a remnant. Trading is conducted at volumes unheard of just a short decade ago, in air conditioned rooms lined with hundreds of high speed servers and void of human personal.

Online trading is huge business. The “players” are quite varied ranging from a guy with a small 10k account sitting at his desktop computer to multi billion dollar investment banks executing high frequency trades. Small traders whimsically hitting the “buy” or “sell” button on their platform are invariably the losers as their decision making abilities are no match for alogrithmic trading. “Algorithmic trading, also called automated trading, black-box trading, or algo trading, is the use of electronic platforms for entering trading orders with an algorithm which executes pre-programmed trading instructions whose variables may include timing, price, or quantity of the order, or in many cases initiating the order by a “robot”, without human intervention”[1].

While there are claims of consistent success by some small traders utilizing algo trading, the game is played differently by high stakes players armed with a warchest of funding. Their algorithms focus on High-frequency trading (HFT). “High-frequency trading is a type of algorithmic trading, specifically the use of sophisticated technological tools and computer algorithms to rapidly trade securities. HFT uses proprietary trading strategies carried out by computers to move in and out of positions in seconds or fractions of a second”[2].

One such derivation of HFT employed by management teams of anonymous trading groups is a form of “front running” trading which is illegal. However, the savvy culprits leave little trace and rake in huge daily profits virtually risk free. This offshoot was revealed on CBS “60 Minutes” news show which aired March 30, 2014 [3]. Briefly, the algorithms used monitor buy and sell orders from other customers. This information is then used by beating a customer to the file server where trades are executed. For instance, if a “slower” customer wants to buy 30,000 shares of IBM, the front running high speed trader will quickly execute that order and then sell it back to the slow customer at a higher price.

Who benefits from these networks? The groups that have the megabucks to build them. We don’t know who they are because the system is so complex they leave no tracks. In the 60 minutes story cited above, a high speed fiber optic network expert was able to figure out what was occurring, but not who owned the network structure being used by these high speed highway robbery traders. Part of the network connected futures exchanges in Chicago to exchanges in New Jersey with fiber optic cable. The cost was $300,000,000 and improved network speed by 3 milliseconds. Overall, tens of billions of dollars were spent on these networks.

The employees of these trading groups benefit as well. I remember a conversation I had with a Cornell faculty member a few years ago; he told me that many of the best and brightest math and engineering students were being siphoned off by Wall Street firms to design networks and algorithms for HFT. No body can blame them for grabbing a job with a salary in excess of $300,000 per year; but when the best and brightest are busy spending their energy doing nothing more than making somebody else richer, versus actually creating something, the “loser” becomes society as a whole
